Note: 1 (Bio)

Jurend "Jerry" Miller (1832–1908)
Jurend Miller, born on January 24, 1832, in Ostfriesland (then part of Germany or Holland), was the son of Ailt Miller and Alberta Klut. Originally spelled "Mueller" in German, the family name became "Miller" after immigration to America, a change that remained consistent through generations.
In 1851, at the age of 19, Jurend immigrated to the United States with his brother Martin. The two young men settled in Ridott Township, near Freeport, Illinois. No other members of their family made the journey from Europe. Martin eventually married and lived in Lennox, South Dakota, with his wife, Lena. Though they had no children, they were fondly remembered by later generations for their kindness and simple hospitality.
While in Ridott Township, Jurend met Ecke Bohning, a young immigrant girl who had also come from Holland or Ostfriesland. Born on November 23, 1842, Ecke immigrated at the age of 14 with her parents, Joe (Göhe) and Wilhelminia Bohning. Tragically, Wilhelminia died during their arduous 13-week sea voyage and was buried at sea. Ecke and her father eventually settled in Ridott, where she met Jurend. They were married in 1860 when Ecke was 18.
Jurend and Ecke began their life together in Ridott, where their first three children were born. In 1868, the family moved west to Grundy County, Iowa, where they lived for thirteen years and welcomed six more children. Then in 1881, seeking new opportunities, they moved once again—this time to the Dakota Territory. They homesteaded a farm six miles southwest of Lennox, South Dakota, where their final four children were born.
Jurend and Ecke were hardworking pioneers who raised thirteen children—all of whom survived to adulthood, married, and had families of their own. The couple’s deep bond and devotion to family defined their lives. After Ecke’s passing on March 23, 1904, from breast cancer at age 61, Jurend moved into the town of Lennox with their youngest daughter, Annie. He lived there until his own death on September 1, 1908, at age 76, due to a heart condition.
Jurend was remembered as cheerful, sociable, and warm-hearted—always ready for a bit of fun with his children and grandchildren. According to family stories, he was much like several of his sons: full of humor, affection, and easy conversation. Ecke, in contrast, was known for her quiet strength, gentle spirit, and unwavering devotion to her family. She lived a modest life, always content with what she had, never complaining, and never spending on herself.
The legacy of Jurend and Ecke Miller lives on not only through their many descendants but also through the memories they left behind. Their tenth child, Jurena Miller Temple, authored "The Miller Book," preserving the stories of her parents and siblings for future generations. Their homestead, once a modest pioneer farm, became the Klinkhagen farm in later years. Though the original buildings and schoolhouse are gone, the land they once cultivated continues to be a symbol of their hard work and enduring family values.

Miller Family Tree Introduction

Miller Family Tree Introduction
Jurend Miller, son of AiIt and Albertha Klut Miller was born on January 24, 1832 in Germany. The " Miller " name received this spelling sometime along the line in the United States. In German it was spelled “Mueller.” But we have always written "Miller", and I will leave it that way. Jurend (Jerry)came to the United States in 1851, with his brother Martin. None of the rest of the family came from Germany. Evidently, the boys stayed together because Martin married, and he and Aunt Lena, in the last years, lived in Lennox and died there. No children. Both Delmar and I remember going over to "Lena Tant's" (she lived right south of us in Lennox) and talking to her in broken English and half "Plat duetch", and she always gave us candy or peanuts. I understand we “brought” her the mumps too in her old age. Many times we went over with soup or pie for her, from home. I don't remember Uncle Martin at all. Mother didn't remember when he died. The brothers came to Ridott township near the settlement of Freeport, Illinois: There Jurend Miller met Ecke Bohning.

Ecke Bohning (pronounced I-Key) came to this country at the age of 14, from Holland. She was born November 23, 1842 in Holland. She came with her parents, Joe and Wilhelminia Bohning. The Dutch spelling looked something like this, "Greau and Vilhelmemeo Bohning.” It helps if you can say Joe in Plat duetch. Some one thought grandfather Bohning’s name was George but both Aunt Jurena and Mom said it was Joe. Her mother, Wilhelminia Bohning, died and was buried at sea. The voyage took 13 weeks. Mother always said her grandfather lived with them till his death, and that he lived to a ripe old age. Another family says that the old man that lived with them was an uncle, Ecke's brother. Another family says that the old man that lived with them was not relative at all, just someone from Holland that stayed with Ecke and Jurend and lived to be 97 Years old.
In one old Bible, Ecke's father's name is listed in Dutch and his birth date is 1803, and the date of his death is 1895. An old man (he was referred to as that by everyone), lived with Jurend and Ecke until his death, is remembered by all. A house full of thirteen children had room for one more. I will leave it at that.
The first stop of Ecke and her father was Ridott township Illinois, east of Freeport. There she met Jurend Miller and two years later, at the age of 18, they were united in marriage. The year 1860. They lived in Ridott township for eight years. The first three children were born there: Ailt Wilhelmina and Albertha. In 1868 they went west and settled in Grundy County, Iowa. They lived there for 13 years. Joe, Albert, Martin, Hannah, Andrew and Helmer joined the family.
In 1881 they moved to Dakota Territory. They settled on the homestead, six miles southwest of Lennox. The last four children, Jurena, William, Eiko and Annie were born here. Ecke (Ida or Agnes) Iived the rest of her life here and passed away in 1904, at the age of 62, after suffering for a year with cancer of the breast. After her death, Jurend Miller moved into the town of Lennox, with the youngest daughter, Annie. Four years later he passed away on September 1, 1908, of a heart condition, at the age of 76. To this union were born thirteen children. None of them died in infancy, but all lived to manhood and womanhood, married and had families. Did you notice that the first four children, Ailt, Albertha, Wilhelminia, and Joe were named after the four grandparents? And it must have been, with a twinkle in his eye, Grandpa named a girl Jurena, after himself, and a boy Eiko, after Ecke. I asked Mother what Grandpa Miller was like' and she answered smiling, "Oh, he was just like Uncle Bert and Eiko and Andrew and Bill (in that order).

He always liked to have a little fun and always had time for some foolishness with the kids. He loved everybody, and it was real easy for him to talk to people. He kept everyone around him happy no matter what."
"What then, what was Grandma like?"
"She was quiet and devoted to her husband and family. I never heard her complain about anything. She never reaily had anything, but she made the best of it, and I don't suppose she ever spent five cents on herself. She was just happy to be where she was and with what she had. Grandma was never cross".
Mother seemed to have the idea that the boys were like their father, and the girls like their mother, and that is the way it should be. You can decide for yourself if you have any Miller or Bohning traits. It has been written "the successful man is one that has lived long, laughed often and loved much. One who left this world better than he found it and whose memory is a benediction.” Let us just say, Grandpa and Grandma, Jurend and Ecke Miller, were successful.
The old buildings of the old homestead are gone. Now there is a new house, barn and out buildings, all modern It is now the Klinkhagen farm. The oid schoolhouse is gone, but a new building on the same location still has school every day.
The old First Turner County Presbyterian church is gone, but a new structure and manse are there and the church is still going and growing: and may the good begun by the Miller's in the 1B00’s go on through each succeeding year. Jurend and Ecke were the "salt of the earth". May the salt "not lose its flavor" in the coming generations.

My sincere thanks to all those who helped compile this information. Many letters were written, not only by myself, but by others. I wish you could all have read the letters. I wish I could have included the college degrees, the businesses represented, the armed service records, a description of the farms and farming records, the homes, the personalities, the talents, the church work, the devotion and loyalties and love of all the people here named. There is much to be proud. It would fill several books. I only hope this record will help to keep the “Millers” mindful of one another. My apologies for errors, Mom will know that I did my best. May God Bless and Keep You tiIl we meet again.
-Jenola Cramer Mundhenke
